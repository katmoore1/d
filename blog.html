How To Legally Blackmail The Owner Of An Etsy Shop

How a new scam targeting Etsy shops cost our family over $91,000 (and nearly bankrupted our boutique)

Note: This document has been updated several times since it's original publication to reflect changes in our legal battles. My apologies for any confusion; all errors are my own.
JR

Kat and I have three reasons for blogging about our experience:

  1. First and foremost, other shop owners need to know theyre being targeted and what to do about it. The results of an informal survey Kat did on Facebook suggested that our recent experience with this blackmail scam was not at all unique. Yet despite that fact, not a single shop owner I spoke with seemed comfortable discussing their struggles publicly. This silence, in my opinion, is a serious problem. The reason being that the majority of shops who havent yet been targeted have not taken the steps necessary to protect themselves and their families. Please note: if you're a shop owner who's already spent time working with a lawyer to file for trademarks and related legal protections for your shop, then please don't waste your time reading further. More than likely, you're safe. However, if you're an owner who hasn't at very least filed for a trademark, then please please continue reading. (Especially if you're a female owner running a shop with under 5000 sales. Based on our conversations with other victims, this seems to be the demographic the scammers are targeting the most.) At bare minimum, please look at the steps you can take to protect yourself we've outlined at the very end of this page.
  2. I also hope to briefly point out what I believe is a pernicious loophole in U.S. Federal Law 15 U.S.C. 1125. (Given that Im not a lawyer, however, my primary focus will obviously be on the first point above.)
  3. Lastly, our attempts to contact journalists and news outlets with our story have thus far been unsuccessful. That said, if you are a publisher reading this, please contact us at katmoore1@protonmail.com.

Before I describe our fight against the scammers, Kat suggested I explain a little about our background for new readers who don't know us personally or haven't visited our shop before.

(If you just want to learn how about this fraudulent scheme and how to protect yourself, please dont feel bad  just skip ahead now. Or, if youre already struggling with this scam, scroll to the bottom for helpful resources and advice.)

So my wife Kat and I live in a small town just south of Atlanta where I work as a civil engineer and she as an 8th grade pre-algebra teacher.1 Our experiment with Etsy began in March of 2013, just three months before the birth of our twin baby girls, when we first decided to open a shop  BabyBooties as we called it at the time. Like many Etsy-ers, our hope was to make enough money to where Kat could eventually stay at home with the girls full-time.

As you might have guessed from the name "BabyBooties," my wifes original idea was to make custom footwear specifically for kiddos under 18-months old. I was skeptical at the time but fast forward half a year and our little boutique was making ~4080 sales per month  more than enough to allow Kat to quit her job and stay home. Honestly we felt really blessed by it all.

And this continued, more or less, for three years. While the business grew steadily, thankfully it never became too much for us to handle alone. Yet because Kat was able to slowly raise her prices, towards the end of 2016, BabyBooties was basically making more per month than I did at work. We weren't getting "rich" or anything but honestly it was just a great situation all around...

In the first two months of 2017, however, things rapidly began to change. (I'll explain how in a bit.) It all started, we think, around January 11th when we first received a strange customer complaint about our "disgusting and offensive website..." (This will make sense soon.)

It's still shocking to me how fast things happened... Within 11 months of our first contact with the scammers, our shop was completely bankrupt and we were tens of thousands of dollars in debt from two sham lawsuits.

Okay let me take a step back (and a deep breath) and explain in really precise terms how this Etsy scam works. Then afterwards, Ill describe how best to fully protect yourself and your shop.

Based on our conversations with other victims, heres how Kat and I think fraudulent companies like QiSys (the company that targeted us) setup the first part of the scam:

  1. The first thing to know is that the scammers target a very particular type of shop. Specifically, they find owners who 1) havent trademarked their shop name by filing with the USPTO, and 2) dont own the .COM domain name that matches their shop name. (for example, in our case: BabyBooties.com) The reasons for this will be explained in a moment.
  2. Next, the scammers do one of two things. If the .COM domain is available to be purchased, they buy it. Or, if someone besides the shop owner owns the domain, they collaborate with its existing owners. Either way, they do this in order to launch a new website on the domain name. (https://www.BabyBooties.com")
  3. Next, once this scam site has been launched, the scammers use professional SEO tools to boost the sites ranking in search engine results. (Apparently this is quite easy because of the importance that Google and Bings search algorithms assign to close word similarities between search queries like Baby Booties and domain names like BabyBooties.com.) As a result, within a few weeks, this scam site is usually the first search result people see when they search the internet for information about your shop. (The fact that the scammers use the .COM domain extension is also significant. Both Google and Bing heavily favor .COM domains in ranking search results  as opposed to .ORG, .CO, etc.)

Practically speaking, that means when people search the web looking for your shop or products, instead of seeing a link to your Etsy page, they see a very credible-looking scam site first. (https://www.BabyBooties.com)

Important note: Google and Bings search results are personalized based on your unique search history so the results you see when you search your shop name wont be the same as what your customers see. Unfortunately this personalization, for various reasons, actually tends to make it more difficult for shop owners to find scam sites that are visible to others. To see if your shop's .COM domain has been registered by someone else, it's easier to check the government's official "WHOIS records" here: https://whois.icann.org.

Now to be honest, the first time I saw the scammers site ranked above our own Etsy page, I remember being frustrated but not terribly alarmed. Looking back, I think I assumed that most people found our Etsy page by first navigating to Etsy.com and then searching for our shop name or products there. Personally I didnt use a search engine like Google to find us on the web so I just assumed that no one else did. (Most shop owners assume the same...)

If you take one thing away from this, take this: that assumption is terribly wrong.

We learned the hard way  people search google for everything. Trust me, not only do your customers use Google and Bing to find your actual Etsy store  they also search the web looking for coupons, return policies, products, reviews, phone numbers, cheaper prices, credentials, email, addresses  you name it, they use Google first. (Just ask your mom how she finds your Etsy page)

That's why a high-ranking scam site will heavily damage your businesseveryone will see it and click on it thinking it's yours.

To give you a clearer sense of how devastating a new scam site can be, consider a few numbers. Just four weeks after our scam site first launched, the total visitors to our Etsy page had dropped by ~37%. And after battling this scam for six months, I estimated that according to 45 important metrics (e.g. repeat customers, referrals, newsletter signups, etc.) our overall business had shrunk by ~33% year-over-year.

That was infuriating, but it gets much worse.

To understand the second (and much more malicious) part of the scam, just look at the image below. Thats what thousands of our clients, friends, family members, colleagues, and potential customers saw when they visited the scam site with our name plastered all over it. (We sell to new mothers, mind you..)


Clearly these vulgar and offensive ads are part two of the whole setup. Not only do companies like QiSys make upwards of $2 every single time your customers visit the scam site, the products typically being advertised are things like Extendz (a penis enhancement pill for men) or other seedy non-FDA approved supplements.

These particular products, in my view, are selected for two reasons:

  1. They are as close to illegal goods or pornographic images as possible without clearly crossing the line. (The ads in the image above are relatively mild compared to others I saw.)
  2. And most importantly, their vulgarity inflicts maximal reputational damage on the Etsy shop being targeted.

But why try to embarrass someone or degrade the public image of a business?

This is an essential part of the scam. (Its sick.)

See the more embarrassed a shop owner is, the more theyre going to want to take down the site  and the more theyll pay to do it. After two months of nose-diving numbers, that was certainly the case for us. Hoping to avoid a lengthy arbitration process or expensive lawsuit, I figured I would just make a realistic offer for the domain and get the whole nightmare over with. So before emailing QiSys I contacted a professional domain broker to get a fair estimate of the domains value. They suggested that similar names typically sell for anywhere from $750 to $2500.

Pricey but sure.. Still way cheaper than trying to change our shop name or something.

But how much did QiSys want for it?

Try $16,500.

Folks its reputation blackmail, plain and simple.

(Its disgusting really.)

At this point, youre probably thinking that theres absolutely no way this is legal. Thats certainly what we thought. (Though the fact that we hadnt trademarked our shop name almost made us think twice...) But since the attorney I hired at the time claimed we had a strong case  we proceeded to file a complaint and eventually two separate civil suits.

(For those interested in the full legal details, Ive appended a long description of the rulings at the end of this document. Full disclaimer: Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with trademark or defamation disputes.)

So to make a long story short, after ~11 months in court and tens of thousands in legal fees, we came away with nothing. Criminal and civil statutes clearly protect against identity fraud, defamation, trademark infringements, and so on  but some forms of what I call online reputation blackmail successfully exploit an ambiguous good faith clause in Federal Law 15 U.S.C. 1125. That meant no defamation damages, no restitution for lost revenue the judge didnt even award us the d*** domain name.

Please listen to me: if the law wont protect you, you have to take measures to protect yourself and your shop. No one can do this for you; it's up to you.

Understand that this problem isnt about to go away anytime soon. Even though QiSys shut down a few months ago, half a dozen different copycat companies using similar scams targeting shops have already popped up over the last year. (Why Etsy shops? My guess: no trademarks, reputation sensitivity, income, lack of legal resources, frequency with which they are googled by customers, etc.)

Again, if you own an Etsy shop, please take the steps below to protect yourself and your family.

In the following, I tried to condense what Kat and I learned from this nightmare into a few basic recommendations  the first section is for people currently dealing with this or a similar scam; and the second is for people who havent yet been targeted by this scam.

Start here if you have already been targeted by scammers.

  1. So the first thing you need to do is see if the scammers made any mistakes that leave them vulnerable to a lawsuit. To exploit the legal loophole QiSys used requires very careful planning and execution. If the company blackmailing you doesnt pass all the legal tests of good faith that Ive described at the end of this document, you might have a case against them. Also, if youve already filed for a trademark for your shop, its possible to argue for various forms of mark infringement. In my experience, however, most owners are like Kat and I and havent trademarked their name so this isnt really a viable course of action. [Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with defamation or similar disputes.]
  2. A quick way to try to remove the scam site is to file a spam complaint with Google, Bing, and other search engines. Start here: 1) Google (https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/spamreport), and 2) Bing (https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/concern). Results vary but some owners have reported that filing a report significantly lowered the rankings of their scam site.
  3. Another option to try requires identifying the company hosting the scam site. (Search the domain name on this site to find the host: https://www.whoishostingthis.com/) Once youve figured out the company, navigate to their homepage and look for a Report Abuse, File A Complaint, or a similar feedback form. (often its in the footer at the bottom of the homepage) This will allow you to complain about the content (the scam site) being hosted on their servers. When you contact them be sure to include a screenshot of the site along with a detailed description of the problem. Offer to identify yourself any way the hosting company deems appropriate to make your takedown request seem more legitimate. Many hosting companies have anti-spam or anti-fraud provisions written into their Terms of Service and can be very helpful with these sorts of things. Something similar to the process above can be done with the domain name registrar where the scammers registered the domain. (typically this is less successful but its worth a shot) Use https://whois.icann.org/en to identify the domain name registrar for the domain and repeat the process outlined above. In my experience, most companies tend to be quite sympathetic if you can really demonstrate the nature of your situation. No one wants to be affiliated with or supportive of these kinds of overtly illicit activities.
  4. Another, much more difficult and time intensive way to fight this, involves drowning the scam site in the search results. (i.e. pushing the scam site to the 45th page of search results that few people see) To do this requires creating content and profiles on high PageRank sites. (For more information on SEO and how search engines rank sites, read: https://moz.com/beginners-guide-to-seo.) This isnt difficult to do but it can be extremely tedious and time-consuming. For a more details on how to do this yourself, read: https://marketingland.com/9-key-points-cleaning-online-reputation-nightmare-via-seo-121924. For those of you willing to pay for a professional to do this for you, several different companies offer exactly this service. I havent had any experiences with any of the following companies so I cant make a recommendation but a few popular options are: https://www.reputationmanagement.com and https://brandyourself.com.

Start here if you havent yet been targeted by scammers.

  1. First off, from what our lawyers have suggested, there are really two ways of legally protecting yourself from this scam before it hits you. The most obvious one is trademarking your shop name. If we'd filed for a trademark with the USPTO before the scam site went up, our lawyers insisted that we could have successfully sued QiSys for tens of thousands of dollars. [Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with trademark violations or similar disputes.] Apparently trademark law is explicitly designed to protect businesses from exactly these sorts of criminal situations. That said, successfully filing a trademark application is slow and expensive. In our experience, paying a lawyer to do a full WIPO search and then file trademark applications with the various international legal entities required to fully secure a mark costs anywhere from $4,000 to $6,000. (That's obviously why so few Etsy shops are trademarked.) It also took the better part of a year to complete the entire process. But if you want to stop this scam in it's tracks, investing in a trademark is clearly the safest route to go. We don't have any personal experiences with this company but several people have recommended https://www.avvo.com/find-a-lawyer is a good way to find an experienced "intellectual property" lawyer.
  2. The second way to protect your shop ahead of time is less expensive but also somewhat less comprehensive. Basically it involves securing the .COM domain that exactly matches your shop name. (Remember for reasons like SEO, pagerank, and so on, the scammers only use exact matches and .COM) Luckily in some cases, this is really cheap because the domain isn't already owned by someone else. (To see if your shop's .COM domain has been registered by another person, check the government's official "WHOIS records" here: https://whois.icann.org.) Please note: if you find that your domain hasn't been taken by someone else, we highly recommend you register it. Take it from someone who didnt see the point of a domain name, it's not worth the riskowning your domain is by far the easiest and cheapest way to protect yourself from this scam. (It will only cost you like $8/year) In fact, at this point, I'd recommend you stop reading and check the WHOIS records abovethen come back and read further. Alright... So if you were fortunate enough to find your shop's .COM domain available and register it, there's no need to read the rest of this section, just skip to our 3rd point below. For those of you who's domain is already owned by someone else (that was the case for us), please continue reading because your situation is more complicated. Why more complicated? As I explained a while back, the scammers will try to buy the domain from its current owners (or collaborate with them). From what Kat and I have gathered from talking to others, this is actually happening a lot right now. So that means you have to ask yourself a few questions: what will the person who owns your domain do when the scammers contact them? Will they sell the domain? Will they collaborate for a share of the revenue? If you think the answer to either of those questions might be 'yes,' then you still have a serious problem on your hands. But how can you know? Well, here are a few heuristics we've learned that can help. First off, just type the domain name into your browser and look at their site. In our experience, if you see a legitimate website then you probably don't have to worry too much. (Obviously people using the domain for real purposes are less likely to work with the scammers.) If, on the other hand, you find a "For Sale" page when you look at their site, then that's a clearly a bad sign. (Even finding a blank page can be ominous.) Domains already up for sale are ideal targets for the scammers. So what should you do if things look bad? Well hopefully that's not your situation because unfortunately there are no easy answers here. Frankly if you can afford it, your best option might be to contact the domain owner and try to negotiate a deal to buy the domain. Take it from us, it's still much cheaper than dealing with the scam or filing for a trademark. But whatever you do, please be proactive. Don't wait around for the scammers to offer the owners $3000 for your domain, buy it, and then destroy your business and reputation. And please don't ignore this and hope it will go away. If you want to hear how Kat and I's negotiated for our second shop's domain, you can contact us at katmoore1@protonmail.com. We'll try to respond to as many requests as we can reasonably manage.
  3. Next, its crucial to quickly identify when a scam site is launched so you can act immediately before it damages your business. As I mentioned before, this can be difficult even if youre vigilant because of the personalization of search engine results. Free services like BrandYourself, however, can help you monitor your shops online reputation to make sure other people arent seeing things you arent. While it varies from company to company, many of these online monitoring services offer to send you weekly or monthly reputation reports and some even allow you to setup alerts so you get emails when new negative content appears. Various other companies offer similar SERP monitoring services if you arent worried about social media and just want to focus on search engines. There are many different online reputation management products out there, each with a slightly different set of features. (some offer reputation insurance, for instance) Id recommend searching Google for online reputation monitoring to find a product that best fits your shops particular needs and goals. Checking your reputation reports emails is an easy way to stay on top of this problem without too much hassle.
  4. Lastly, educating yourself about the methods these scammers are using can help you spot derivative scam attempts or copycat schemes. It also might be useful to read the recommendations I outlined for shop owners already facing a scam site (above this section) to better understand how it works and what you can do. Above all, keep yourself safe.
