How To Legally Blackmail An Etsy Shop Owner

How a new scam targeting Etsy shops cost our family over $81,000 (and nearly eventually bankrupted our boutique)

Note: This document has been updated several times since it's original publication to reflect changes in our legal battles. My apologies for any confusion; all errors are my own.
—JR

Kat and I have three reasons for blogging about our experience:

  1. First and foremost, other shop owners need to know they’re being targeted and what to do about it. The results of an informal survey Kat did on Facebook suggested that our experience with this new scam was not at all unique. Yet despite that fact, not a single shop owner I spoke with seemed comfortable discussing their struggles publicly. This silence, in my opinion, is a serious problem. The reason being that the majority of shops who haven’t yet been targeted have not taken the steps necessary to protect themselves and their families.
  2. I also hope to briefly point out what I believe is a pernicious loophole in U.S. Federal Law 15 § U.S.C. 1125. (Given that I’m not a lawyer, however, my primary focus will obviously be on the first point above.)
  3. Lastly, our attempts to contact journalists and news outlets with our story have thus far been unsuccessful. That said, if you are a publisher reading this, please contact us at evans77843@gmail.com.

Before I discuss our horrific experience with the scammers, my wife suggested I explain a little about our background for new readers who don't know us personally or haven't visited the site before.

(If you just want to learn how about the scheme and how to protect yourself, please don’t feel bad — just skip ahead now. Or, if you’re already struggling with this scam, scroll to the bottom for helpful resources and advice.)

So my wife Kat and I live in a small town just south of Dallas, TX where I work as a civil engineer and she as an 8th grade pre-algebra teacher. In early 2013, just months before the birth of our twin baby girls, we decided to open our first Etsy shop — “BootiesForBabies” as we called it at the time. Our hope was to make enough money to where Kat could eventually stay at home with the girls full-time.

My wife’s original idea was to make “custom footwear” for infants. Admittedly I was skeptical at first. Yet to the surprise of no one but me, Kat had her first order within hours of launching on Etsy. (In case it’s not obvious: she’s “the talent;” I just do the taxes.) Fast forward another eight months and our little boutique was making ~40–80 sales per month — more than enough to allow Kat to stay home with the girls. Needless to say, we were both thrilled.

This continued, more or less, for three years. While the business grew steadily, it never became too much for us to handle alone. Yet because Kat was able to slowly raise her prices, towards the end of 2016, “BootiesForBabies” was actually making more per month than I did at work. (Operating under a different name by that time.) It's not like we were getting "rich" or something but we were having lots of fun. Honestly it was just a great situation all around...

Enter the scammers.

So at this point, I want to take a step back and explain exactly how this new scam works. Then afterwards, I’ll describe how best to fully protect yourself and your shop.

After dealing with this nightmare for 14 months, here’s how I think unscrupulous companies like ConeFly (the company that targeted us) setup the first part of the scam:

  1. The first thing to know is that the scammers target a very particular type of shop owner. Specifically, they find owners who 1) haven’t trademarked their shop name by filing with the USPTO, and 2) don’t own all the .COM domain names that match their shop name. (for example, in our case “BootiesForBabies.com,” “BootiesForBabiesShop.com,” and so on.) The reasons for this will be explained in a moment. (If you want to see if someone else owns your domains, search your shop name at https://namecheap.com)
  2. Next, the scammers do one of two things. If one of the domain names is available to be purchased, they buy it. Or, if someone besides the shop owner owns the domain, they collaborate with its existing owners. Either way, they do this in order to launch a new website on the domain name. (“www.BootiesForBabies.com")
  3. Next, once this “scam site” has been launched, the scammers use professional SEO tools to boost the site’s ranking in search engine results. (Apparently this is quite easy because of the importance that Google and Bing’s search algorithms assign to close word similarities between search queries like “Booties For Babies” and domain names like “BootiesForBabies.com.”) As a result, within a few weeks, this scam site is usually the first search result people see when they search the internet for information about your shop. (The fact that the scammers use the “.COM” domain extension is also significant. Both Google and Bing heavily favor .COM domains in ranking search results — as opposed to .ORG, .CO, etc.)

Practically speaking, that means when people ‘google’ your shop, instead of seeing a link to your Etsy page, they see a very credible-looking scam site first. (“www.BootiesForBabies.com")

Now to be honest, the first time I saw the scammer’s site ranked above our own Etsy page, I remember being frustrated but not terribly alarmed. Looking back, I think I assumed that most people found our Etsy page by first navigating to Etsy.com and then searching for our shop name or products there. Personally I didn’t use a search engine like Google to find us on the web so I just assumed that no one else did. (Most shop owners assume the same.)

If you take one thing away from this, take this: that assumption is unbelievably wrong.

We learned the hard way — people search google for everything. Not only do your customers use Google and Bing to find your actual Etsy store — they also search the web looking for coupons, return policies, reviews, phone numbers, cheaper prices, credentials, email, addresses — you name it, they use Google first. (Just ask your mom how she finds your Etsy page…)

Note: Google and Bing’s search results are “personalized” based on your unique search history so the results you see when you search your shop name won’t be the same as what others see. Unfortunately this “personalization,” for various reasons, actually tends to make it more difficult for shop owners to find scam sites that are visible to customers.

All that to say, a high-ranking scam site will heavily damage your business.

To give you a clearer sense of how devastating a new scam site can be, consider this. Just four weeks after our scam site first launched, the total visitors to our Etsy page had dropped by ~27%. And after battling this scam for six months, I estimated that according to 4–5 important metrics (e.g. repeat customers, referrals, newsletter signups, etc.) our overall business had shrunk by ~18% year-over-year.

That was infuriating, but it gets much worse.

To understand the second (and much more malicious) part of the scam, just look at the image below. That’s what thousands of our clients, friends, family members, colleagues, and potential customers saw when they visited the scam site with our name plastered all over it. (We sell to new mothers, mind you.)


Clearly these vulgar and offensive ads are part two of the whole setup. Not only do companies like ConeFly make upwards of $2 every time our customers view the ads on the scam site, the products typically being advertised are things like Extendz (“a penis enhancement pill for men”) or other seedy non-FDA approved “supplements.”

These particular products, in my view, are selected for two reasons:

  1. They are as close to illegal goods or pornographic images as possible without clearly crossing the line. (The ads in the image above are relatively mild compared to others I saw.)
  2. And most importantly, their vulgarity inflicts maximal reputational damage on the Etsy shop being targeted.

But why try to embarrass someone or degrade the public image of a business?

This is an essential part of the scam. (It’s sick.)

See the more embarrassed a shop owner is, the more they’re going to want to take down the site — and the more they’ll pay to do it. After two months of nose-diving numbers, that was certainly the case for us. Hoping to avoid a lengthy arbitration process or expensive lawsuit, I figured I would just buy the domain and get the whole nightmare over with. Before emailing ConeFly, however, I contacted a professional “domain broker” to get a fair estimate of the domain’s value. They suggested that similar names typically sell for anywhere from $500 to $2000.

Pricey but sure..

So how much did ConeFly want for it?

Try $15,000.

It’s reputation blackmail, plain and simple.

(It’s disgusting really.)

At this point, you’re probably thinking that there’s absolutely no way this is legal. That’s certainly what we thought. (Though the fact that we hadn’t trademarked our shop name made us think twice.) But since the attorney I hired at the time claimed we had a strong case — we proceeded to file a complaint and eventually two separate civil suits.

(For those interested in the full legal details, I’ve appended a long description of the rulings at the end of this document. Full disclaimer: Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with trademark or defamation disputes.)

To make a long story short, after 14 months in court and tens of thousands in legal fees, we came away with nothing. Criminal and civil statutes clearly protect against identity fraud, defamation, trademark infringements, and so on — but some forms of what I call “online reputation blackmail” successfully exploit an ambiguous “good faith” clause in Federal Law 15 § U.S.C. 1125. That meant no defamation damages, no restitution for lost revenue — the judge didn’t even award us the BootiesForBabies.com domain. (You cannot imagine how furious I was when I eventually capitulated and purchased that damn thing.)

Please listen to me: if the law won’t protect you, you have to take measures to protect yourself and your shop.


Understand that this problem isn’t about to go away anytime soon. Even though ConeFly shut down last month, half a dozen different copycat companies using similar scams targeting shops have already popped up over the last year. (Why Etsy shops? My guess: no trademarks, reputation sensitivity, income, lack of legal resources, frequency with which they are “googled” by customers, etc.)

Again, if you own an Etsy shop, please take the steps below to protect yourself and your family.

Below I tried to condense what Kat and I learned from this nightmare into a few basic recommendations — the first section is for people currently dealing with this or a similar scam; and the second is for people who haven’t yet been targeted by this scam.

Start here if you have already been targeted by scammers.

  1. So the first thing you need to do is see if the scammers made any mistakes that leave them vulnerable to a lawsuit. To exploit the legal loophole ConeFly used requires very careful planning and execution. If the company blackmailing you doesn’t pass all the “legal tests” of “good faith” that I’ve described at the end of this document, you might have a case against them. Also, if you’ve already filed for a trademark for your shop, it’s possible to argue for various forms of mark infringement. In my experience, however, most owners are like Kat and I and haven’t trademarked their name so this isn’t really a viable course of action. [Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with defamation or similar disputes.]
  2. A quick way to try to remove the scam site is to file a spam complaint with Google, Bing, and other search engines. Start here: 1) Google (https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/spamreport), and 2) Bing (https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/concern). Results vary but some owners have reported that filing a report significantly lowered the rankings of their scam site.
  3. Another option to try requires identifying the company “hosting” the scam site. (Search the domain name on this site to find the host: https://www.whoishostingthis.com/) Once you’ve figured out the company, navigate to their homepage and look for a “Report Abuse,” “File A Complaint,” or a similar feedback form. (often it’s in the footer at the bottom of the homepage) This will allow you to complain about the content (the scam site) being hosted on their servers. When you contact them be sure to include a screenshot of the site along with a detailed description of the problem. Offer to identify yourself any way the hosting company deems appropriate to make your takedown request seem more legitimate. Many hosting companies have anti-spam or anti-fraud provisions written into their Terms of Service and can be very helpful with these sorts of things. Something similar to the process above can be done with the domain name registrar where the scammers registered the domain. (typically this is less successful but it’s worth a shot) Use https://whois.icann.org/en to identify the domain name registrar for the domain and repeat the process outlined above. In my experience, most companies tend to be quite sympathetic if you can really demonstrate the nature of your situation. No one wants to be affiliated with or supportive of these kinds of overtly illicit activities.
  4. Another, much more difficult and time intensive way to fight this, involves “drowning” the scam site in the search results. (i.e. pushing the scam site to the 4–5th page of search results that few people see) To do this requires creating content and profiles on high ‘PageRank’ sites. (For more information on SEO and how search engines rank sites, read: https://moz.com/beginners-guide-to-seo.) This isn’t difficult to do but it can be extremely tedious and time-consuming. For a more details on how to do this yourself, read: https://marketingland.com/9-key-points-cleaning-online-reputation-nightmare-via-seo-121924. For those of you willing to pay for a professional to do this for you, several different companies offer exactly this service. I haven’t had any experiences with any of the following companies so I can’t make a recommendation but a few popular options are: https://www.reputationmanagement.com and https://brandyourself.com.

Start here if you haven’t yet been targeted by scammers.

  1. It’s really important to note that if we’d filed for a trademark before the scam site went up, our lawyers have strongly suggested that none of this would be legal. Apparently trademark law is explicitly designed to protect businesses from exactly these sorts of criminal situations. [Obviously this is not professional legal advice and should not be misconstrued as such. Please seek legal counsel from a licensed attorney familiar with trademark violations or similar disputes.] That said, paying a lawyer to do a WIPO search and then file with the USPTO is costly. When we started BootiesForBabies, it wasn’t feasible for us to spend the $1,000-$2000 necessary to hire an attorney. Since then, however, we’ve learned about online companies like LegalZoom and Trademarkia that offer trademark registration services for much cheaper ($150-$400). While we haven’t used either of these services, they might be something worth looking into if you have the funds to register a trademark to protect your shop. 
  2. It’s crucial to quickly identify when a scam site is launched so you can act immediately before it damages your business. As I mentioned before, this can be difficult even if you’re vigilant because of the “personalization” of search engine results. Free services like BrandYourself, however, can help you monitor your shop’s online reputation to make sure other people aren’t seeing things you aren’t. While it varies from company to company, many of these “online monitoring services” offer to send you weekly or monthly “reputation reports” and some even allow you to setup “alerts” so you get emails when new negative content appears. Various other companies offer similar SERP monitoring services if you aren’t worried about social media and just want to focus on search engines. There are many different “online reputation management” products out there, each with a slightly different set of features. (some offer reputation “insurance,” for instance) I’d recommend searching Google for “online reputation monitoring” to find a product that best fits your shop’s particular needs and goals. Checking your “reputation reports” emails is an easy way to stay on top of this problem without too much hassle.
  3. Try to acquire the domain names that match your shop name. Focus primarily on the 1–3 search queries that you think your customers are most likely to type into Google or Bing when they look your shop up. (for example: “Booties For Babies,” “Booties For Babies shop,” “Booties For Babies Etsy,” etc.) Take it from someone who didn’t see the point of a domain name, don’t risk it — owning your shop’s domains is by far the cheapest and most reliable way to protect yourself. Search engines favor .COM domains in search results so worry about that domain extension first. When purchasing your domains, I don’t recommend using GoDaddy or other generic domain registrars — in my experience, it’s better to try https://namecheap.com or other registrars that have experience with scammers and features designed to protect people in this situation.
  4. Educating yourself about the methods these scammers are using can help you spot derivative scam attempts or copycat schemes. It might be useful to read the recommendations I outlined for shop owners already facing a scam site (above this section) to better understand how it works and what you can do. Above all, keep yourself safe.
